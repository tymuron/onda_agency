# Positioning, Value Proposition, Differentiation & Founder Brand

For solo/boutique service studios. Contents: 1 problem · 2 specialization ·
3 Dunford value prop · 4 differentiation · 5 founder brand · 6 hero ·
7 AI-tells · 8 pricing-as-positioning · 9 positioning→SEO/GEO · 10 order.

## 1. The core problem
Services are maximally commoditized — everyone says "premium, fast, custom,
results-driven", so the only remaining buyer axis is price. Positioning's whole
job: give a *specific buyer* a reason to choose you that competitors cannot
truthfully copy.

## 2. Specialization (highest-leverage move; do first)
Specialization is a business decision that makes every later message easier.
Economics: a specialist sees the same problem repeatedly → expertise faster →
advisor not vendor → escapes price competition. Three compounding wins (Enns):
higher win rate, price premium, authority. You don't need everyone (Chris Do:
~10 clients/year, not the market). Narrowing concentrates revenue, doesn't
shrink it; it shifts leads from mostly-unqualified to mostly-qualified.
**Recommended shape for a solo operator: vertical + outcome.** Formula:
`[Outcome] for [vertical] in [geography]`. Keep breadth without diluting: the
hero commits to the niche; a secondary line / separate pages list other
verticals. Don't hedge in the hero. Failure mode: "flinching" — abandoning the
niche during a normal slow patch.

## 3. Value proposition — Dunford's 5 components (run in order)
1. **Competitive alternatives** — not just other agencies; the real alternative
   is Wix / their nephew / a 2015 site / doing nothing. Position against THAT.
2. **Unique attributes** — what only you have (one senior person doing the
   actual work; vertical fluency; trilingual; fixed price).
3. **Value** — translate attributes to the buyer's outcome (more booked
   appointments; look as established as the big competitor).
4. **Best-fit customers** — those who care most about that value.
5. **Market category** — the frame that makes value obvious ("patient-booking
   websites for clinics", not "web design agency" = commodity frame).
Rule: **state outcomes, not features.** "Premium website" is a feature;
"more patients booking online without you lifting a finger" is value.

## 4. Differentiation in a commodity space
Generic adjectives are unclaimable (if a competitor can say the sentence
unchanged, it differentiates no one). Replace with two copy-proof things:
- **Proof-led:** specific, falsifiable results — real names, numbers,
  before/after ("Clínica X: 4→19 online bookings/month in 90 days").
- **Opinionated point of view:** a stance competitors won't take ("Most clinic
  sites are digital brochures. A clinic site has one job: turn a visitor into a
  booked appointment."). A POV repels wrong-fit, magnetizes right-fit — exactly
  what a solo operator wants.

## 5. Founder / personal brand (the solo operator's unclaimed advantage)
People trust people, not faceless logos; personal brands start differentiated.
The killer line a 30-person agency cannot truthfully say: **"You work directly
with me — the person who designs and builds your site. No account managers, no
juniors, no handoffs."** Say **"I", not "we"** when it's one person (pretending
bigger is dishonest and discards the advantage). Put the founder's face, name,
first-person story on the homepage + About. Trust = specificity (city, years,
exact count of sites shipped, named clients, the real process). This
structurally answers the #1 SMB fear ("will I be handed to a junior?").

## 6. Hero messaging
Answer fast: who-for + outcome + mechanism + proof. Formulas:
`[Outcome] for [specific buyer] — [mechanism/differentiator].` /
`[Result] in [timeframe], without [pain].`
- ❌ "Premium websites for businesses that need to look serious." / "We help
  businesses grow with stunning, results-driven web design." / "🔥 Only 2 spots
  left."
- ✅ H1 "Websites that turn visitors into booked patients — for dental &
  aesthetic clinics in Spain." · sub "I'm [name]. I design and build the whole
  site myself — no agency layers. Fixed price, live in ~3 weeks." · proof strip
  "Clínica X +240% bookings · Clínica Y booked 2 weeks out."
Cut: countdown timers, "X spots left", "trusted by businesses worldwide",
generic laptop hero, "in today's digital landscape", "unlock your potential".

## 7. Generic / AI-sounding tells → fix
Tells: vague abstractions, all-"we", unprovable adjectives, fake scarcity, no
named customers, no numbers, no person. Fix = read-aloud test + use the
customer's own words from real sales conversations ("I need more patients", "my
site looks old"), not marketing language.

## 8. Pricing transparency = positioning
For SMB buyers, showing price is differentiation and pre-qualifies leads.
Publish productized tiers (fixed scope + published price). Structure so ~70-80%
fit a standard package; "custom" only for true outliers.

## 9. How positioning feeds SEO/GEO
AI/Google embed the query and match semantically to pages whose
**category + who-for + outcome** are explicit. Vague positioning = no query to
match; specific positioning = you become the literal answer to "best website
designer for dental clinics in Spain". Put category+vertical+geography+outcome
in H1, title, meta, and an About line in buyer-query language. AI favors
specific entities + GBP + named citable results.

## 10. Priority order
1 choose niche → 2 rewrite hero (who-for+outcome+solo mechanism, kill urgency)
→ 3 add named proof w/ numbers → 4 founder front-and-center ("I", photo, story)
→ 5 publish productized pricing → 6 add POV section → 7 align title/meta/About
to buyer-query language.
